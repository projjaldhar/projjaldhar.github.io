Overview

The California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA) expanded on CCPA to give California residents stronger data rights — including the right to correct inaccurate personal information, limit use of sensitive data, and opt out of cross-context behavioural advertising. For Verizon Connect, this meant building a formal privacy portal that could handle data subject requests, consent management, and preference capture at scale.

Rather than treating compliance as a minimum viable legal checkbox, this project designed the privacy portal and notification panel as a genuine user experience investment — resulting in an 8.3% monthly engagement lift as users who had previously ignored notifications engaged more when given meaningful control over what they received.

The Problem

CPRA compliance deadline

California's expanded privacy regulations required a formal, auditable mechanism for users to exercise their data rights — and a deadline that couldn't move.

Notification fatigue

Users were receiving broad, undifferentiated platform notifications — leading to low engagement and growing opt-out rates that reduced the platform's ability to communicate with customers.

No consent preference management

Users had no way to selectively control which types of platform communications they received — it was all or nothing, pushing many to opt out entirely.

Solution & Architecture

The project delivered two connected components: a CPRA Privacy Portal handling data subject requests (access, deletion, correction, opt-out of data sharing), and a Notification Preference Panel allowing users to granularly manage which platform notifications they received. Both were designed to be accessible, transparent, and genuinely useful — not just legally defensible.

Key Features

  • CPRA data subject request portal — self-service interface for users to submit, track, and receive responses to access, deletion, correction, and opt-out requests within regulatory timelines
  • Granular notification preferences — category-level notification controls allowing users to opt in or out of specific communication types without affecting all notifications
  • Automated request routing and SLA tracking — internal workflow for handling data subject requests with automated assignment, deadline tracking, and audit trail for compliance reporting
  • Consent-aware notification engine — the platform notification system updated to check user consent preferences before delivery, ensuring only opted-in users receive each notification category
  • Compliance audit trail — full history of consent changes, data request submissions, and responses stored for regulatory audit purposes

Impact & Outcomes

The platform achieved CPRA compliance on schedule. The more surprising outcome was the 8.3% monthly engagement lift from the notification panel — users who had been ignoring all notifications began engaging when they could choose which types they received. Giving users control, rather than reducing it, proved to be the more effective strategy for maintaining communication reach.

This project reinforced a broader product principle: compliance features don't have to be purely defensive. Designed well, they can improve the product experience and user trust simultaneously — and the engagement data made this case with hard numbers.

Lessons Learned

  • Compliance and UX are not in conflict — the instinct to treat privacy features as a legal task rather than a product task leads to poor experiences. Treating this like any other user-facing feature — with research, iteration, and usability testing — produced better compliance outcomes and better engagement.
  • Granularity increases consent rate — users who are given a binary opt-in/opt-out choice often opt out to avoid all communications. Category-level controls allowed users to preserve the notifications they valued, which increased overall consent rates compared to the previous all-or-nothing approach.
  • Legal and product need to be aligned from day one — late legal feedback on portal language and request flow required rework. Involving legal in early wireframe reviews rather than final copy reviews would have saved two sprints of revision work.
  • The audit trail is a feature, not an afterthought — engineering initially scoped the audit logging as a backlog item. Prioritising it to the first release made the difference between a defensible compliance record and a gap that would have required retroactive data reconstruction.
