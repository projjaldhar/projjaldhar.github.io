Project 04

StoryGlobe —
3D Gateway to Global Folklore

An interactive 3D globe where you explore folk tales, oral traditions, and legendary stories from cultures around the world — tap any country flag to step inside its stories.

Three.js React 18 TypeScript Supabase Mobile-first
14 Countries & cultures
3D WebGL globe in the browser
0 Sign-up required
Live Deployed on Vercel

Overview

Folk tales, oral traditions, and legendary stories are among humanity's oldest forms of knowledge-sharing — but they've never had an immersive, globally connected home on the web. StoryGlobe changes that by turning the Earth itself into a navigation interface. Instead of searching or scrolling, users spin a 3D globe, land on a country, and are immediately drawn into that culture's stories and storytellers.

The app is fully mobile-first, requires no account, and rewards curiosity with a Passport badge system that tracks which countries you've explored and how many stories you've read. Every detail — from the serif typography to the illustrated tale pages — is designed to make reading feel like an event, not a chore.

The Problem

Stories siloed by culture

Global folklore lives in scattered books, Wikipedia stubs, and paywalled databases — there's no single immersive place to explore it all.

Flat, scroll-based UX

Lists and search boxes strip the sense of discovery. Reading about another culture should feel like travel, not a database query.

Signup friction kills curiosity

Requiring an account before showing content is the fastest way to lose a curious user who just wants to read one story.

Solution & Architecture

The globe is the interface. A Three.js sphere with a real Earth texture serves as the homepage — country flags are pinned at their real-world coordinates, and dragging the globe to find them is itself an act of discovery. Tapping a flag opens a bottom drawer with that country's stories and authors; tapping a story leads into a full illustrated reading experience.

Key Features

  • Interactive 3D globe — WebGL-rendered Earth with drag-to-spin, pinch-to-zoom, and country flag markers pinned at real-world coordinates
  • 14 countries of folklore — curated folk tales, oral traditions, and legendary stories with cultural context, moral, and origin for each
  • Passport badge system — tracks which countries you've visited and stories you've read, rewarding curiosity without requiring an account
  • No sign-up required — progress is persisted in localStorage so users jump straight into stories without any friction
  • Mobile-first design — responsive camera logic adapts FOV and Z-distance to portrait and landscape screens; bottom drawer feels native on a phone
  • Privacy-first analytics — Vercel Analytics tracks country_opened and story_opened events with no third-party cookies or consent banners

Impact & Outcomes

StoryGlobe is live at storyglobe.vercel.app. Vercel Analytics tracks two key custom events — country_opened (which cultures users are most curious about) and story_opened (which stories get read vs. skipped) — providing a clear picture of engagement across the full funnel, from globe to reading view.

The product decision to remove login entirely — relying on localStorage for progress — proved critical. Drop-off at the entry point was minimal, validating the hypothesis that curiosity-driven products live or die by how quickly users can reach their first moment of value. The Passport system provided the retention hook without the cost of an auth flow.

Lessons Learned

  • WebGL performance on lower-end mobile is a first-class constraint — the globe ran smoothly on modern devices but stuttered on older phones until texture resolution was reduced and geometry complexity was managed. Mobile performance testing should start on day one, not before launch.
  • The no-login decision validated quickly but surfaced a new problem — localStorage progress can't sync across devices. Power users who switched from phone to desktop lost their Passport progress. A lightweight, optional account sync would be the first thing I'd add in a next iteration.
  • Cultural content requires more schema design than expected — attribution, moral, cultural context, and tone all needed their own structured fields. A flat content model would have made filtering and future personalisation much harder.
  • Gamification is worth the scope — the Passport badge system took longer to build than estimated but became the most mentioned feature in user feedback. Retention hooks designed for curiosity-driven products need to reward exploration, not just completion.
