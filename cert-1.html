AI Product Strategy & Build Lab

A hands-on programme where product practitioners skip the theory and actually build working AI products — from zero to deployed, alongside a cohort of peers.

The Curious PM AI Product Strategy & Build Lab is not a lecture series. It's a structured build sprint where PMs are expected to ship a real, working AI product by the end of the cohort — and defend the decisions behind it.

I joined the March 2026 cohort, alongside 17 other product practitioners from across SaaS, fintech, and enterprise software. The programme runs over several weeks, with each week focused on a different phase: problem framing, AI tooling selection, MVP scoping, building, and finally — presenting to a panel.

What the Programme Covers

The curriculum is deliberately practitioner-led. Rather than abstract AI theory, the focus is on the decisions product managers actually face when working with AI teams: how to evaluate model trade-offs, how to design for uncertainty in AI outputs, how to write effective specifications for LLM-based features, and how to define success metrics that account for non-deterministic system behaviour.

Topics covered included:

AI product strategy — positioning AI capabilities as product features, defining where AI creates genuine value versus where it adds noise, and how to build a product roadmap that accounts for model iteration cycles.

Prompt engineering for product teams — how to collaborate with engineers on prompt design, how to evaluate prompt quality systematically, and how to maintain consistency across a product surface that relies on language model outputs.

Build lab — using tools like Cursor, Claude, and Replit to prototype and deploy functional AI products without deep engineering resources. The emphasis is on velocity: go from idea to working demo within days, not sprints.

What I Built

My cohort project focused on fleet intelligence — a problem domain I know deeply from my work at Verizon Connect. I built a working AI assistant that could answer natural-language queries about fleet performance data, surfacing insights that previously required a data analyst to extract manually.

The project forced me to make real architectural decisions: which model to use, how to handle hallucination risk in a domain where accuracy matters, and how to design the user experience so that fleet managers — not AI researchers — could trust and act on the output.

Why It Matters

The most important thing I took from Curious PM was a shift in how I think about AI product ownership. Building something real — even a prototype — changes how you ask questions of your engineering team, how you write requirements, and how you evaluate whether an AI feature is actually working. You stop talking about AI in the abstract and start talking about latency, evaluation sets, and fallback behaviour.

For anyone in product who wants to be credible in the AI space, the ability to build — even imperfectly — is becoming a baseline expectation. This programme gave me that foundation.
