From OMS Consultant to Product Owner — What Actually Transferred

The skills that crossed over, the mental models that needed rebuilding, and why deep implementation experience is an underrated advantage for a first-time PM.

I spent nearly a decade as a consultant implementing IBM Sterling OMS for enterprise retailers. Staples, Kohls, Williams Sonoma — complex, high-stakes systems where getting it wrong meant orders not getting fulfilled and customers going elsewhere. When I made the move to product management, people asked me two questions: "What's the same?" and "What surprised you?"

Both questions have more interesting answers than I expected.

What Crossed Over Directly

Systems thinking. OMS implementations live or die on understanding how data flows between systems — how an order from a storefront becomes a fulfilment instruction in a warehouse becomes a shipment event in a carrier system. That kind of end-to-end mental model is exactly what product managers need when they're defining requirements that touch multiple services. I could draw integration diagrams on day one.

Client-side empathy. Consulting is a forcing function for understanding what stakeholders actually need versus what they say they need. I spent years translating "we need X" into "you're experiencing Y problem, and here are three ways to solve it." Discovery interviews felt familiar.

Tolerance for ambiguity in complex systems. Enterprise software is full of undocumented edge cases, legacy decisions that made sense at the time, and constraints you only discover in production. PMs who've only worked in greenfield environments sometimes struggle with this. I was used to it.

What Needed Rebuilding

The definition of "done." As a consultant, done meant the system behaved according to the specification. As a PM, done means users are getting value. These are different things and the gap between them is where most product failures live. It took me longer than I'd like to admit to stop treating specs as the finish line.

Saying no. Consultants are trained to be responsive. A client asks for a feature, you scope it, you build it. PMs who carry that instinct into product roles end up with bloated roadmaps and teams building things nobody asked for in any meaningful strategic sense. Learning to say "that's interesting — but not now, and here's why" was a real adjustment.

Operating without a requirement document. Consulting work usually starts with a statement of work, a requirements document, a project plan. Product is much more comfortable with uncertainty at the start. The question "what should we build?" feels different when you're the one who has to answer it, rather than the one who receives the answer.

The Underrated Part

The thing nobody talks about: having built things makes you a fundamentally different kind of PM in rooms where engineers are present. I can spot when a requirement I've written is going to create an ugly edge case. I can have an honest conversation about technical debt because I've lived in codebases that were paying for the sins of previous teams. Engineers trust PMs who've shipped — not because they need to write code, but because they understand what it costs.

If you're a consultant or engineer thinking about moving into product: the transition is harder than you think in some ways, and easier in others. But the implementation experience isn't baggage to leave behind — it's leverage, if you learn how to use it.
