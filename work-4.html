Overview

Airline retail has traditionally been limited to what fits in an onboard trolley. Home delivery represented a completely new revenue channel — allowing passengers to browse a broader catalogue during their flight and receive purchases at home after landing. But enabling it required solving a complex operational puzzle: connecting in-flight ordering to ground logistics without any existing infrastructure.

As Product Owner, I designed and delivered the end-to-end platform from zero — spanning passenger-facing ordering, crew fulfilment workflows, post-flight order routing, and last-mile delivery integrations — and then managed the rollout across multiple airline clients with their distinct operational requirements.

The Problem

No home delivery OMS for airlines

No off-the-shelf platform handled the unique combination of in-flight ordering, crew handling, customs constraints, and post-flight last-mile delivery.

Disconnected crew and logistics workflows

Cabin crew, ground handlers, and delivery partners operated in silos with no shared order visibility — making fulfilment coordination a manual, error-prone process.

Multi-airline complexity

Each airline had different crew systems, baggage policies, customs rules, and last-mile partners — requiring a flexible platform rather than a single rigid implementation.

Solution & Architecture

The platform was designed as a multi-tenant OMS with airline-specific configuration layers. A passenger-facing ordering interface (accessible via seatback or mobile) fed orders into a central management system, which routed them to crew tablets for in-flight handling, then passed confirmed orders to ground logistics and last-mile delivery partners after landing.

Key Features

  • In-flight ordering interface — mobile-optimised catalogue and checkout designed for low-connectivity cabin environments, with offline-capable order queuing
  • Crew management tablet app — real-time order dashboard for cabin crew covering order acceptance, packing confirmation, and payment processing
  • Multi-carrier last-mile integration — configurable delivery partner connectors enabling each airline to use their preferred ground logistics and postal networks
  • Customs and duty management — automated duty calculation and customs documentation generation based on destination country and product category
  • Post-flight order tracking — passengers receive delivery confirmation and tracking link automatically, closing the loop on the purchase experience

Impact & Outcomes

The platform was successfully deployed across multiple European airline clients, enabling a home delivery revenue channel that had not previously existed in their retail operations. Each airline rollout validated the multi-tenant architecture — airlines were onboarded with their specific configurations without requiring platform rebuilds.

The zero-to-one nature of this build — designing for a customer journey that spanned the air and ground with no existing playbook — required close collaboration with airline operations, cabin crew unions, customs authorities, and delivery partners simultaneously, making it one of the most complex stakeholder environments I've navigated in a product role.

Lessons Learned

  • The crew experience is the product's critical path — a perfect passenger-facing UI meant nothing if crew couldn't manage orders efficiently at 35,000 feet. Investing in the crew tablet workflow early, with real cabin crew in usability sessions, prevented the most expensive failures.
  • Offline-first design is non-negotiable in-flight — cabin connectivity is unpredictable. Discovering this during testing rather than design required significant rework. Every ordering and crew flow needed to function without a live connection.
  • Customs complexity scales with ambition — the broader the product catalogue, the more complex the duty and restriction matrix. Scoping the initial catalogue to low-duty, non-restricted categories made the first airline launch significantly faster.
  • Multi-client products need configuration, not customisation — early airline clients wanted bespoke features that would have forked the codebase. Holding the line on a configurable core saved long-term maintenance cost and made subsequent client onboarding much faster.
